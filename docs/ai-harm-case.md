EPIC SEPSIS MODEL CASE STUDY

The Epic Sepsis Model (ESM) is a machine learning system developed by Epic Systems to predict which hospital patients are at risk of developing sepsis. The intention was to identify patients early so that clinicians could intervene sooner and improve patient outcomes. However, an external validation study conducted by Wong et al. (2021) found that the model performed significantly worse than expected. The researchers reported that approximately 67% of sepsis cases were missed by the model, demonstrating poor sensitivity and raising concerns about its reliability in real clinical settings.
The primary reason for this failure was that the model was implemented across hospitals without independent external validation. Epic largely relied on internal testing to assess the model's performance before deployment. Internal validation can show that a model works well on the data it was trained on, but it does not guarantee that the model will perform equally well in different hospitals with different patient populations, clinical practices, and data collection methods. As a result, the model failed to generalise when used in practice.
What was not anticipated was that hospitals would place significant trust in Epic's published performance metrics without questioning whether those results would apply to their own patient populations. There was an underlying assumption that because the model had been developed by a major healthcare software provider, its reported accuracy would transfer across different clinical environments. In reality, differences in patient demographics, disease prevalence, and hospital workflows meant that the model's performance varied considerably. Independent external validation before widespread deployment could have identified these weaknesses and allowed the model to be recalibrated for different healthcare settings.
Additional external verification would likely have identified the model's poor discrimination and sensitivity before it was introduced into routine clinical practice. The JAMA study highlighted that the model failed to accurately distinguish between patients who would and would not develop sepsis. Detecting these shortcomings earlier would have allowed developers to improve the model through recalibration, retraining with more representative datasets, or adjusting decision thresholds before the system was relied upon for clinical decision-making.
The case also demonstrates both technical and human failures. From a technical perspective, the model's poor calibration meant that many high-risk patients were not identified, while some low-risk patients triggered unnecessary alerts. From a human factors perspective, these false positive alerts contributed to alert fatigue. When clinicians are repeatedly exposed to alerts that prove to be inaccurate or unnecessary, they gradually lose trust in the system and become more likely to dismiss or ignore future alerts. As a result, even genuine sepsis warnings may be overlooked, delaying diagnosis and treatment for patients who urgently need care. This combination of inaccurate predictions and reduced clinician trust significantly weakened the effectiveness of the system.
This case provides an important lesson for the Mercer project. Similar risks could arise if our AI model is trained using a limited or unrepresentative dataset or relies on proxy labels that do not accurately reflect real-world outcomes. Like the Epic Sepsis Model, our system should undergo independent external validation using data from multiple sites before deployment. This helps ensure that the model performs consistently across different environments, reduces the risk of bias, and improves both accuracy and user trust.
